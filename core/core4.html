Neuroplasticity

“Morale and grade points took a leap when students understood the idea that intelligence is malleable. Not only did those students who already believed this do better in school, but when researchers actively taught the idea to a group of students, they performed significantly better than their peers in a control group.” - Kali Trzesniewski and Carol Dweck.

Neuroplasticity gives people the knowledge that they can learn and breaks down road-blocks by providing a pathway to follow that enables you to learn. Although the brain isn’t a muscle, it is similar to a muscle in that it will become stronger if it is put to use in a healthy way. If someone thinks they will fail before they even try, they will fail. If someone gives up after failing, they will never learn. Failure allows us to pinpoint the things we need to work on and learn in order to succeed.

How I Engage with Neuroplasticity

I’ve always thought that with time, effort, focus and access to relevant materials, anyone can learn anything. The real question for me has always been whether or not I want to spend time learning something. I might not be particularly interested in a certain subject, but later in life that subject is connected with something I am actually interested in, so I take the time to learn it. I need a reason to want to learn something and won’t just spend time learning something just to prove I can, because I already know I can.

On the other hand, I always jump headfirst into something that I don’t completely yet understand because I know I will learn it. I learn what I need to when I need to, sometimes rather quickly when I’m under the pump!

Ways to Increase Neuroplasticity

Kleim and Jones outlined ten principles of neuroplasticity:

  1. Use it or lose it – engage in the activity in order to keep fresh.
  2. Use it and improve it – practice makes perfect.
  3. Specificity – pinpoint what you need to work on and focus on it.
  4. Repetition matters – there isn’t a magical amount of time needed, but going over the same exercises regularly promotes growth.
  5. Intensity matters – focus your attention and limit distractions.
  6. Time matters – make sure you’re learning what you need to know when it’s the most beneficial for you to learn and know it.
  7. Salience matters – know what is you want to achieve and why.
  8. Age matters – the sooner, the better!
  9. Transference or Generalization – ask yourself “in what other areas of my life might this be beneficial?”
  10. Interference – are there any habits you need to unlearn? Is there any confusion that you need to clear up before moving forward?

Growth Mindset

Growth Mindset is the idea that we can grow our brain’s capacity to learn and to solve problems. This is relevant to everything and everyone. We are constantly evolving and growing, this is enhanced when we believe we can grow and learn. We need to be open to the idea of failure being a way to learn and grow rather than failure being an indicator of incapability.
